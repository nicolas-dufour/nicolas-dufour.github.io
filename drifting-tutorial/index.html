What does “drifting” mean?

Teach the generator its next move.

1sample current
2make a target
3fit the generator
generateddatanext target

repeat during trainingqθ₀ → qθ₁ → ··· → p

Drifting is a training loop. First sample the current generator. Second, the drift field turns each output into a slightly better target. Third, update theta so the same noise seeds map close to those targets. Repeat: the pushforward q_theta moves toward p, and the sequence of moves is stored in the weights. At test time we do not replay this trajectory; the final generator produces a sample in one call.

The scale dilemma

One τ cannot both reach and resolve.

small τtoo local to see the datano signal
large τsees both modes at oncemode blur
many τcoarse move → fine correctionboth
0.20 · reach0.05 · shape0.02 · detail
A narrow kernel preserves local geometry, but when generated and real distributions are far apart it assigns almost zero weight and supplies no useful direction. A broad kernel reaches across that gap, but averages over distinct modes and points toward their midpoint. The implementation avoids choosing: it computes one drifting loss for each temperature 0.02, 0.05, and 0.20, then sums them. The broad loss supplies reach; the narrow losses progressively recover shape and detail.

Why the destination is a rest point

Same expected neighborhood. Zero net vote.

At p equals q, attraction and repulsion are expectations over the same neighborhood distribution. They therefore have the same expected mean-shift and cancel. Mini-batch estimates can still be noisy; this is an equilibrium in expectation. The converse direction is addressed later.

For class-conditional ImageNet training, the paper draws N-pos real positives from the same class as c. The label supplies the semantic neighborhood for free, and the ablation shows that more positives materially improve FID. In another modality the condition may be continuous, compositional, or weakly labeled, so “same class” may not define a useful pool. The general drifting framework is broader than labels, but transferring this strong conditional recipe requires designing an analogous positive relation—through labels, retrieval, or a learned metric. That extra design and supervision is a real portability cost.

Kernel choice · reopen the obvious candidate

Why not use a Gaussian kernel?

why can we prove it? The Gaussian drift has a hidden score-matching form.
The original model preferred a Laplacian kernel empirically. We ask the most natural alternative: what if the neighborhood were Gaussian? It is smooth, analytically convenient, and—most importantly—we can prove identifiability: if the Gaussian drift vanishes everywhere, the two distributions are equal. The reason is not merely that Gaussians are nice. The resulting drift turns out to be an exact difference of smoothed scores.
Step 1 · turn samples into a density

One point → one Gaussian bump. Add them up.

samples from p
one bump per sample
their sum: pσ
Gaussian-smoothed data density
same recipe for generated samples
Start with only the data distribution. Replace every data sample by a Gaussian bump of bandwidth sigma. Adding those bumps gives p-sigma, the Gaussian-blurred data density. Nothing about scores yet. The exact same construction applied to generated samples gives q-sigma. These are simply the two kernel density estimates already hiding in the drifting denominators.
Step 2 · read the attractive drift

The same Gaussian gives a height and a slope.

attraction toward data
denominator = height
numerator = slope
slope ÷ height = score
Now inspect one half of the drift: attraction toward data. Its denominator is exactly the height of the blurred density p-sigma at x. For a Gaussian, displacement times weight is the derivative of the same Gaussian bump. After averaging, the numerator is sigma-squared times the slope of p-sigma. Divide slope by height and we obtain the log-density slope—the score. So one Gaussian mean-shift term is exactly one smoothed score.
Step 3 · repeat for q, then subtract

One blue score. One orange score. Their difference is the drift.

data attraction
model mean shift
drift = attraction − model shift
The second half is identical: apply the same Gaussian calculation to generated samples and obtain the score of q-sigma. The drifting operator was defined as data attraction minus the model mean shift. Therefore the full drift is sigma-squared times the data score minus the model score. This is the score-matching connection in one line, after deriving each term separately.

Standard score models parameterize a vector field and train a network to approximate the data score. Sampling then repeatedly evaluates that learned field. Gaussian drifting uses the same mathematical object in a different place. The data batch gives a non-parametric score of p-sigma; the generated batch gives a non-parametric score of q-sigma; the kernel drift is their exact residual. That residual updates the sampler during training. At inference the scores disappear and only f-theta remains. This also explains identifiability: zero residual means equal smoothed scores, hence equal smoothed densities, and Gaussian convolution is injective, so p equals q.

Identifiability · part 1 of 2

Zero drift makes the log-density ratio flat.

the identity we just proved
① zero drift means zero slope
② a flat ratio can still be a constant
③ but both curves contain one unit of mass
There is a small but important step hidden in the phrase “equal scores.” The drift is the gradient of the log-density ratio. If it vanishes, that ratio has no slope, so p-sigma can only be a constant multiple of q-sigma. The constant is not arbitrary: both are probability densities and integrate to one. Therefore the constant is one and the two blurred densities are identical.
Identifiability · the last step

Gaussian blur shrinks. It never erases.

⇒ ⇒
Assume p and q still differ. Gaussian blur can make that difference tiny, but its multiplier is always positive, so the difference cannot disappear exactly. Yet the previous slide proved that the blurred difference is exactly zero. Therefore the original difference is zero and p equals q. Keep the Fourier statement in the paper.
Kernel choice · near the solution

The model can be wrong at several scales.

Near the solution, the remaining difference between model and data is a mixture of broad shape errors, medium structural errors, and fine detail errors. Fourier analysis is just a way to watch one such scale at a time. In the paper's local linearized analysis, every scale decays with its own clock. The kernel chooses those clock speeds.
Identifiable does not mean fast

A fixed Gaussian hears one band clearly.

fastest bandthe update rate peaks here
fine modesstill visible, but the update is exponentially tinypractically frozen
why Laplacian helpedits heavier spectral tail keeps fine modes louderpolynomial slowdown
This resolves the apparent contradiction with injectivity. A Gaussian multiplier is never zero, so no information is erased. But the learning rate is not the multiplier alone: it is proportional to sigma squared k squared times the Gaussian spectrum. That creates a band-pass learning window peaking at square-root-two over sigma. Frequencies far above the window receive an exponentially weak update. The Laplacian kernel has a heavier spectral tail, which helps explain its empirical strength.
The fix · move the sweet spot

Start wide. Shrink σ. Reveal finer errors.

large σglobal shape
medium σparts and modes
small σedges and texture
every scale gets a turn
Sigma is a scale knob. A large bandwidth places the fastest-learning window at coarse frequencies. Lower sigma moves the window right, toward finer frequencies. Exponentially shrinking sigma makes the best-heard frequency grow exponentially, so equal time is spent per octave. In practice the schedule stops at a positive sigma-min chosen for the finest desired resolution.

It learned to erase its own update, not to match the data. With stop-gradient, drift norm and sample error remain tightly coupled. The paper gives the formal frozen-field and JKO justification.
Part 4 · deriving new drift operators

Hand-balancing attraction and repulsion is not a recipe.

The original attraction-minus-repulsion balance works, and anti-symmetry guarantees cancellation at the target. But small manual coefficient changes cause large empirical failures. More importantly, anti-symmetry only checks a proposed operator; it does not tell us what new operator to construct or what objective it decreases. We need a construction rule.
Recipe · step 1

First choose a meter that reads zero only at the data.

choose a sufficiently regular discrepancya single number for how wrong q is
moving toward p lowers the meterfar → closer → matchedthe destination is built into F
one correct zerono false target distribution
possible geometriessmoothed KLMMDSinkhorn
Start by deciding what mismatch means. Choose a sufficiently regular discrepancy on distributions that is nonnegative and has its unique zero at the data law. The discrepancy is a meter: it tells us whether q is closer, and it gives the correct destination. Regularity and existence of a smooth first variation are the technical assumptions; keep them in the paper and notes rather than on the main slide.
Recipe · steps 2–4

Differentiate the meter into local arrows.

1choose Fglobal mismatch meter
2differentiatewhere would moving mass lower F?
3make the drift
4freeze + fittarget x + τVF(x), then stop-grad
Now turn the global meter into local motion. The functional derivative asks how F reacts to placing probability mass at x. Its spatial gradient tells that mass which direction lowers F most quickly; the negative gradient is the drift. Evaluate those arrows at today's generated distribution, freeze the targets, and fit the generator. This is the reusable recipe: choose, differentiate, freeze, fit.

Optional background · 1/2 · optimal transport

Move one cloud into the other—with the least work.

Optimal transport starts with two distributions viewed as piles of mass. A transport plan says how much mass travels from each generated location to each data location. Every move costs distance times mass. Among all plans that deliver the correct amount everywhere, optimal transport chooses the one with the least total work. Watch the crossed, expensive plan untangle into a short matching.
Optional background · 2/2 · soft transport

Sinkhorn makes that plan soft, fast, and differentiable.

1softennearby pairs receive larger weights
2balancenormalize rows, then columns—repeat
3resulta smooth transport plan we can differentiate
main resultoptimal transportSinkhorn↑
Exact optimal transport can produce a hard, non-smooth assignment. Sinkhorn adds a small entropy preference, so mass can spread across several nearby destinations. Computationally, we form a soft matrix of pair weights and alternately rescale its rows and columns until the prescribed source and target masses are satisfied. These simple matrix-scaling iterations are fast, stable, and differentiable.
